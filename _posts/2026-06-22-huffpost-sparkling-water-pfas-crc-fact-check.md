# HuffPost Sparkling Water–Colorectal Cancer Article: Source Audit and Evidentiary Review

**Date:** 2026-06-21  
**Researcher:** researcher  
**Article reviewed:** ["Can Sparkling Water Increase Your Risk Of Colorectal Cancer?"](https://www.huffpost.com/entry/colorectal-cancer-sparkling-water-goog_l_6a33f495e4b0dfdabcebc21f), HuffPost Life, by Julia Ries Wexler, published 2026-06-21  
**Review objective:** Follow every linked study, compare actual findings against the article's narrative, and assess evidentiary support.

---

## Executive Summary

The HuffPost article asks whether canned sparkling water increases colorectal cancer (CRC) risk via PFAS leaching from can linings. The article's **text body is more honest than its headline** — both interviewed specialists say there is no direct evidence and PFAS levels in sparkling water are far below known hazard thresholds. But the framing (headline, lede, social-media hook) strongly implies a risk the cited science does not support.

**The evidence chain has a broken link at every step:**

- [x] PFAS are generally carcinogenic → True, but the established cancer links are renal, testicular, and thyroid — **not colorectal**.
- [x] PFAS found in some sparkling waters → True (Consumer Reports 2020), but at trace levels: most brands <1 ppt, well below EPA's 4 ppt MCL and IBWA's 5 ppt guideline.
- [x] PFAS can disrupt the gut → True in vitro, but at concentrations **500,000× higher** than observed dietary exposure.
- [x] Chronic inflammation → CRC → Well-established general pathway, but never demonstrated for PFAS at dietary exposure levels.
- [ ] PFAS exposure → CRC → **"Limited and inconclusive"** per the single most relevant study cited: a 2025 scoping review (PMC12661558) that is the only comprehensive evidence synthesis on this exact question.
- [ ] Canned sparkling water → CRC → **Never studied. No evidence exists.**

**Bottom line:** The article is not fabricated, but it is a classic HuffPost pattern: take a genuine scientific question with weak evidence, wrap it in an alarming headline, and let the "experts say there's no proof" caveats in paragraph 14 do the journalistic CYA. The article's own experts and its most authoritative cited source both directly contradict the implied threat.

---

## 1. The Article's Narrative Chain

The article constructs the following implied causal pathway:

```
Canned sparkling water → can linings contain/leach PFAS → PFAS exposure
→ gut inflammation & microbiome disruption → colorectal cancer
```

It supports this with:
- **2 specialist interviews** (Dr. Maen Abdelrahim, GI Medical Oncology; Dr. Steven Lee-Kong, Colorectal Surgery)
- **7 external links** (6 scientific, 1 consumer testing)

Below, each linked source is evaluated for what it actually demonstrates vs. what the article claims it demonstrates.

## 2. Source-by-Source Audit

### Source 1: PMC9905956 — Ultra-Processed Foods and CRC Precursors

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Cited for** | "Growing evidence suggests deli meats, alcohol, sugar-sweetened beverages and ultra-processed snacks may contribute to colorectal cancer." |
| **What it actually studies** | Association between ultra-processed food (UPF) consumption and colorectal cancer **precursors** (adenomas, serrated lesions) in 142,052 participants across 3 prospective cohorts. |
| **Relevant finding** | Higher UPF consumption associated with ~18–20% increased odds of CRC precursors (OR 1.18–1.20, 95% CI fully above 1.0). |
| **Does it study PFAS?** | **No.** Never mentions PFAS, sparkling water, or can linings. Studies UPFs as a category (Nova classification). |
| **Does it study sparkling water?** | **No.** |
| **Verdict** | **Context-setting only.** Establishes that diet matters for CRC risk. Has zero evidentiary bearing on the sparkling-water–PFAS–CRC question. Cited honestly in the article as general background. |

---

### Source 2: EPA — PFAS Health Effects Overview

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Cited for** | "Research has linked PFAS to a higher risk of several types of cancer, including kidney and testicular cancer, along with impaired immunity, liver damage, infertility and thyroid disease." |
| **What it actually says** | EPA's official summary of PFAS health effects based on human and animal studies. Links PFAS to: reproductive effects, developmental effects, increased risk of some cancers (prostate, kidney, testicular), reduced immune function, thyroid disruption. |
| **Does it mention colorectal cancer?** | **Not in the linked page.** The cancers highlighted are kidney, testicular, prostate, and thyroid. |
| **Does it mention sparkling water or food packaging?** | Not as a specific exposure pathway on this page. |
| **Verdict** | **Accurate but incomplete.** The article correctly reports the cancers EPA links to PFAS. But it omits that CRC is notably absent from the EPA's listed cancer concerns. The article uses this source to establish "PFAS = carcinogenic" and then pivots to CRC, which the source does not support. |

---

### Source 3: Consumer Reports (via skimresources redirect) — Bottled Water PFAS Testing

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Cited for** | "Some studies have found PFAS in popular sparkling water brands, but the levels often fall well below the current federal advisory limits." |
| **What it actually is** | Consumer Reports investigation from **2020** (6 years old). Tested 47 bottled waters (35 noncarbonated, 12 carbonated) for 30 PFAS compounds and 4 heavy metals. |
| **Key finding** | Most carbonated waters had detectable PFAS. Several brands exceeded CR's preferred 1 ppt threshold, but all were well below the federal voluntary limit of 70 ppt and most state limits (12–20 ppt). Topo Chico had the highest levels. |
| **What it actually demonstrates about the CRC question** | **Almost nothing.** It shows some sparkling waters contain trace PFAS. It does not study health outcomes, does not estimate cancer risk, and does not evaluate can-lining leaching specifically (PFAS could come from source water, not packaging). |
| **Article's handling** | The article cites this honestly — it explicitly says levels "fall well below the current federal advisory limits." But the CR report is from 2020, predating EPA's 2024 enforceable MCLs (4 ppt for PFOA/PFOS). |
| **Verdict** | **Tangential but accurate.** Establishes PFAS *presence* at trace levels. Does not establish risk. |

### Source 4: PMC11209830 — PFAS Exposure and Inflammatory Bowel Disease

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Cited for** | "Evidence suggests PFAS may disrupt the intestinal barrier. Over time, these disruptions can cause gut inflammation, damage to the intestinal lining and changes to the gut microbiome." |
| **What it actually studies** | Nested case-control study (n=75, U.S. military personnel) examining whether serum PFAS mixtures measured years before diagnosis are associated with later-onset IBD (Crohn's and ulcerative colitis). |
| **Key finding** | PFAS mixture associated with ~2× odds of IBD (OR 2.13 for CD, 1.76 for UC) for samples drawn up to 10 years before diagnosis. PFAS concentrations comparable to general U.S. population levels. |
| **Strengths** | Repeated prediagnostic measurements over 10 years; first study to include emerging PFAS in IBD analysis. |
| **Limitations** | Small sample (n=75); semi-quantitative PFAS measurement; WQS regression prone to overfitting; not designed to establish causation. |
| **Does it study CRC?** | **No.** Studies IBD, which is a CRC risk factor, but this is one link removed from the claimed outcome. |
| **Does it study sparkling water?** | **No.** Studies serum PFAS from all exposure sources. |
| **Verdict** | **Legitimate scientific finding that supports one link in the chain** (PFAS → gut inflammation/IBD). But the IBD→CRC link is a separate question not addressed by this study, and sparkling water as a specific exposure pathway is absent. The article uses this appropriately as mechanistic plausibility evidence, not as direct proof. |

---

### Source 5: PubMed 41038555 — PFAS Disrupt Gut Microbiome in MetS Model

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Cited for** | Changes to the gut "microbiome" (linked alongside the IBD study). |
| **What it actually studies** | In vitro (host-free) Human Colonic Model examining how PFAS exposure affects gut microbiota composition and metabolism in a metabolic syndrome context. |
| **Key finding** | PFAS at 100 and 1,000 ng/mL altered gut microbiota composition (notable shifts in *Morganella* and *Bilophila* genera), altered SCFA production, and changed metabolite profiles. |
| **Critical caveat** | This is an **in vitro model with no human host**. The PFAS concentrations tested (100–1,000 ng/mL = 100–1,000 ppb) are **orders of magnitude higher** than what a person would ingest from sparkling water (where total PFAS is typically <1 ppt = <0.001 ppb). The exposure gap is roughly **100,000× to 1,000,000×**. |
| **Verdict** | **Mechanistic plausibility only, at irrelevant concentrations.** Demonstrates PFAS *can* perturb gut biology, which is useful background. Does not demonstrate that dietary PFAS levels from sparkling water *do* perturb the gut in living humans. The article does not mention the concentration gap. |

---

### Source 6: PMC8889855 — Inflammation and Colorectal Cancer (Review)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Cited for** | The well-established link between "chronic inflammation" and CRC. |
| **What it actually is** | A review article (2022, *Annals of Gastroenterological Surgery*) summarizing the general biological mechanisms linking chronic inflammation to CRC: inflammatory markers (CRP, NLR), transcription factors (NF-κB, STAT3), cytokines (TNF-α, IL family), and miRNAs. |
| **Does it mention PFAS?** | **No.** |
| **Does it mention sparkling water?** | **No.** |
| **Does it mention any environmental chemical?** | No — it is a pure cancer biology review. |
| **Verdict** | **Valid general science used as a rhetorical bridge.** The article uses this to say "inflammation → CRC is established" (true) and then implies "PFAS → inflammation → CRC" (not established by this source). This is the classic "A→B and B→C, therefore A→C" chain where **both links must be independently verified**, not just one. |

### Source 7: PMC12661558 — PFAS and CRC Scoping Review (THE KEY SOURCE)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Cited for** | PFAS may "promote tumor growth" via alterations to intestinal cells. |
| **What it actually is** | A **2025 scoping review** published in *Environment International* — the only comprehensive, systematic evidence synthesis on PFAS exposure and colorectal cancer specifically. Reviewed 26 studies (3 in vitro, 6 animal, 12 epidemiological, 5 reviews) following PRISMA-ScR guidelines. |
| **Key finding — stated conclusion** | **"Evidence on the relationship between PFAS exposure and CRC risk remains limited and inconclusive."** |
| **In vitro (3 studies, high confidence)** | PFOS and PFOA promote CRC cell proliferation and migration at 1 nM–10 μM concentrations. Consistent across all 3 studies. |
| **Animal (6 studies, mixed findings)** | Some studies show increased intestinal tumors (Hansen 2019, POP mixture). Others show *decreased* tumors (Wimsatt 2016, 2018 — PFOS actually reduced tumor count/size in APCmin and PDX models). Some show no effect (Ngo 2014). Findings are **inconsistent in direction**. |
| **Epidemiological (12 studies, mixed, mostly low confidence)** | Varying associations: some positive (Messmer 2022, RR 1.49 for colon cancer), some **inverse** (Innes 2014 — PFOS OR 0.24, meaning PFAS associated with *lower* CRC odds), some null (Alexander 2024, HR 1.03 with CI crossing 1.0). The review explicitly notes: "some studies reporting positive, inverse, or null associations." |
| **How the article uses it** | The article cites this review only for the narrow claim that PFAS may "promote tumor growth" — cherry-picking the in vitro findings while omitting the review's own conclusion that the overall evidence is "limited and inconclusive," that animal studies show *mixed direction* of effect, and that epidemiological findings are inconsistent. |
| **Verdict** | **The article's most serious omission.** This is the single most relevant source cited, and its central finding directly contradicts the implied threat. The article acknowledges the review exists but only extracts the pro-risk mechanistic finding while burying the "inconclusive" verdict. |

## 3. The Headline–Body Gap

The article operates on two levels simultaneously:

| Level | Message | Supported? |
|-------|---------|------------|
| **Headline + lede + social hook** | "Can sparkling water increase your CRC risk?" + "If you sip on canned sparkling water regularly, listen up." | Implies risk deserving of concern. |
| **Body text (doctors quoted)** | "No direct evidence… far below those shown to cause cancer… not considered a significant source of PFAS exposure." | Honest about the evidence gap. |
| **Body text (bottom line)** | "No direct, conclusive evidence… not currently considered a major source of PFAS exposure." | Accurate. |

This is the HuffPost formula: write a scientifically honest article, then put an alarming question as the headline. The headline does the viral work; the body text does the journalistic CYA.

**Both interviewed experts say, in their own words:**
- Abdelrahim: "Based on current evidence, **canned sparkling water is not considered a significant source of PFAS exposure**" and "the amount leached from beverage cans specifically is **far below** those shown to cause cancer in experimental studies."
- Lee-Kong: Levels "**fall well below** the current federal advisory limits" (while noting the science is evolving and low levels don't guarantee safety — the precautionary principle).

## 4. The Missing Evidence

What would be needed to support the implied narrative, but does not exist:

| What's needed | Status |
|---------------|--------|
| Epidemiological study of sparkling water consumers and CRC incidence | **Does not exist** |
| Measurement of PFAS leaching from can linings into carbonated water at realistic storage times/temperatures | **Not cited; limited published data** |
| Demonstration that dietary PFAS levels (~1 ppt range) produce gut inflammation in humans | **Not demonstrated** |
| Consistent epidemiological association between PFAS exposure and CRC (across multiple studies) | **Not found** — the scoping review reports mixed, inconsistent findings |
| Evidence that reducing sparkling water consumption reduces CRC risk | **Does not exist** |

## 5. The Article's Origin: Responding to Social Media Hysteria

The article itself admits the concern originated from "Instagram and TikTok" where "influencers claim the beverages… contain undisclosed chemicals and high levels of forever chemicals that cause cancer." HuffPost is not creating the scare — it's **responding to an existing viral panic** by interviewing doctors. But its packaging (headline, lede) amplifies rather than resolves the panic, and it omits the strongest contrary evidence from its own sources.

## 7. Recommendations

1. **Social media:** The article's own experts and best cited source contradict the scary headline. The evidence for sparkling water → CRC is essentially nonexistent. The article is a response to viral TikTok claims, dressed in health-journalism clothing.

2. **If you're evaluating HuffPost as a source generally:** This article follows a predictable pattern — scientifically honest body text, alarming question-as-headline, omission of the most exculpatory evidence from the best cited source. It's not fabrication; it's manipulative framing.

3. **If you're concerned about PFAS exposure:** The established major exposure pathways are contaminated drinking water (especially near industrial/military sites), certain occupations (firefighting, chemical manufacturing), and some food packaging (greaseproof paper, fast-food wrappers). Canned sparkling water is, per the article's own experts, "not considered a significant source." Filter your tap water; that's where the bulk exposure comes from.

4. **The precautionary principle argument:** Lee-Kong's point that "even low levels do not guarantee safety" is scientifically valid — absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. But this is true of almost everything and is not a reason to single out sparkling water. The same logic applies to tap water, air, soil, and most food packaging. The question is relative risk, and sparkling water ranks very low.
