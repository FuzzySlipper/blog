# Evaluation: Wired's "The Painful Truth About Long COVID"

**Researcher:** researcher agent  
**Date:** June 27, 2026  
**Article:** "The Painful Truth About Long Covid" by Alan Levinovitz, Wired, June 1, 2026  
**Scope:** Evaluate grounding of claims, source quality, omissions, and framing bias

---

## Executive Summary

The article is **skilled narrative journalism that fails on scientific grounding**. It tells a compelling human story while systematically omitting or minimizing the biomedical evidence that contradicts its central thesis — that Long COVID is best understood through a mind-body/neuroplastic lens, and that patient advocacy has created a "climate of fear" that suppresses this truth.

**Grounding score: LOW (2/5).** The article's load-bearing claims either do not survive contact with the literature or are framed in misleading ways. The author is a professor of religion (not medicine) promoting a forthcoming book. Key biomedical findings — including a Cell paper proving causal autoantibodies published two weeks before the article — are absent.

The article belongs to a recognizable genre: the contrarian longread that positions itself as "the truth they won't let you discuss." This framing creates false symmetry between a well-evidenced biomedical literature and a mind-body approach whose intellectual lineage traces to John Sarno's unvalidated "tension myositis syndrome."

---

## Article Summary

Levinovitz argues:
1. After $2 billion and six years, biomedical Long COVID research has yielded "little more than hypotheses"
2. A BMJ systematic review gives "moderate evidence" for CBT and exercise as treatments
3. Mind-body approaches ("brain retraining") are effective but silenced by patient advocates
4. The patient community, scarred by the PACE trial controversy, has created a "climate of fear" that prevents open discussion
5. The CDC declaring "ME/CFS is a biological illness" represents a paradigm that excludes psychological factors
6. Dr. Becca Kennedy and Dr. Howard Schubiner's neuroplastic symptom framework offers a way forward

The article is built around a single patient narrative ("Andrew Larson") who recovered through Kennedy's brain retraining program.

## Key Claims vs. Evidence

### Claim 1: "$2 billion yielded little more than hypotheses"

**Verdict: Misleading — omits a mature biomedical literature**

The RECOVER Initiative received ~$1.15B initially, plus $662M in 2024, allocated through FY2029. It is ongoing. Key findings omitted from the article:

| Evidence | Publication | What it shows |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| Functional autoantibodies → causal animal model | de Sá, Silva et al., *Cell*, May 2026 | IgG from LC patients transferred to mice induced fatigue, pain, nerve damage, and balance loss — **causal-mechanistic proof** |
| Multi-organ excess risk | Al-Aly group, *Nature Medicine* 2022, *Lancet Diab Endo* 2022 | Large cohort studies (n > 100k) quantifying cardiovascular, renal, neurological sequelae |
| Reproducible immune signature | Klein, Wood, Jaycox et al. (Iwasaki/Putrino labs), *Nature* 2023 | Cortisol depression, EBV reactivation, T-cell exhaustion, autoantibody profiles |
| Brain structural change | Douaud et al., *Nature* 2022 | UK Biobank pre/post MRI: 0.2–2% greater gray-matter loss after mild infection |
| Integrative multi-omics | Tasoula et al., *Frontiers in Immunology*, May 2026 | OXPHOS suppression + chronic immune activation across tissues; molecular overlap with ME/CFS biopsies |
| Prevention RCT | Bramante et al., *Lancet Infect Dis* 2023 | Metformin at acute infection reduced LC incidence by ~41% |
| Bioenergetic impairment | Davenport et al., 2026 | Two-day CPET shows objective PEM in both ME/CFS and LC |

The *Cell* 2026 autoantibody paper was published two weeks before the Wired article. It is not mentioned. This is a significant omission — it represents the strongest causal evidence to date and directly undermines the article's "nothing to show" framing.

**Caveat:** RECOVER's clinical treatment trials have indeed been disappointing. RECOVER-VITAL (March 2026) found Paxlovid does not improve established LC. RECOVER ENERGIZE found metformin fails as a treatment (though it works for prevention). The article could have fairly criticized the clinical trial program without erasing the basic science.

---

### Claim 2: "BMJ systematic review gives moderate evidence for CBT and exercise"

**Verdict: Methodologically laundered — the underlying evidence is weak and contested**

The BMJ review (2024) rates CBT and "physical and mental health rehabilitation" as having "moderate certainty evidence." However:

- **The CBT recommendation rests on a SINGLE trial** (a Dutch study) at high risk of bias due to lack of blinding
- **Objective outcomes were null:** The CBT trial measured physical activity with body monitors; participants did NOT move more after treatment. The investigators chose not to report these null results in the main paper. David Tuller calls this "a likely example of research misconduct."
- **The exercise recommendation uses the REGAIN trial**, which *The BMJ itself had already corrected* in May 2024 for overstating its applicability — the trial only studied hospitalized patients but generalized to all LC patients
- **NICE (UK) formally withdrew graded exercise therapy for ME/CFS in 2021** due to evidence of harm; the 2021 guideline states GET "should not be offered"
- **Of 24 trials in the review, 21 gave no actionable evidence**
- **The PACE trial (2011)**, the intellectual foundation of GET/CBT for post-infectious fatigue, showed ~7–8% recovery across ALL arms when analyzed against its own pre-registered endpoints

The BMJ review's abstract claims REGAIN "probably" improves symptoms without noting participants were hospitalized — this crucial limitation appears "only deep in the text." David Tuller, who is quoted in the Wired article, has been the primary critic of this review.

---

### Claim 3: "Mind-body approaches are effective but silenced"

**Verdict: Unsupported by clinical trial evidence; presented with selective success stories**

The article profiles Dr. Becca Kennedy and Dr. Howard Schubiner's ATNS (Association for the Treatment of Neuroplastic Symptoms) approach. Key facts not disclosed:

- **Schubiner is a direct inheritor of John Sarno's "tension myositis syndrome" framework** — a controversial mind-body theory for chronic pain that has never been validated with biomarkers in decades
- **No RCT evidence** for brain retraining in Long COVID exists. The article presents a single success story ("Andrew Larson") and implies this generalizes
- **The Finnish Gupta Program trial** (a structured mind-body intervention) showed "no significant differences in effectiveness between the intervention arms" in interim results — the article does not mention this
- **The Lightning Process trial** (2017, *Archives of Disease in Childhood*) was a methodological disaster — recruited 56% of participants before registration, swapped outcomes, issued a 3,000-word correction — also not mentioned
- **RECOVER's Neuro cognitive trial** (2025) found that cognitive retraining programs (BrainHQ, Cognitive Recovery) and tDCS brain stimulation ALL failed in LC

The article critiques patients for seeking unproven biomedical treatments while giving a pass to unproven mind-body interventions. This asymmetry is not acknowledged.

**Fair point:** Some patients DO report improvement with brain retraining. The article's patient narrative may be genuine. The problem is presenting a single anecdote as vindication of a framework while dismissing an entire biomedical literature as "hypotheses."

---

### Claim 4: "Patient advocacy has created a climate of fear"

**Verdict: Contains a kernel of truth but creates false symmetry**

The article extensively documents online harassment of researchers (Wessely, Sharpe, and others received death threats). This is real and should not be minimized.

However, the article frames patient opposition to GET/CBT as primarily ideological — patients "need" a biological narrative for identity reasons — rather than as a rational response to:
- **74% of PACE trial participants** reporting that exercise made them worse
- **NICE formally withdrawing GET in 2021** based on evidence review
- **The PACE trial's own pre-registered endpoints** showing ~7–8% recovery across all arms (i.e., the treatments didn't work even by the trial's own standards)
- **Systematic patient surveys** consistently showing high rates of harm from exercise-based interventions

The psychotherapist Fred Rossi (a patient himself) put it well: "So the community is not a mob protecting a story. It is a population that watched a treatment paradigm hurt its own members and now flinches when the same machinery rolls out under a new name."

The article does not include a single concrete account of harm from brain retraining, while giving extensive space to harms from patient advocacy.

---

### Claim 5: "CDC's 'biological illness' framing excludes psychological factors"

**Verdict: Misleading — the CDC's stance reflects evidence, not ideology**

The CDC states "ME/CFS is a biological illness." The article frames this as a paradigm that silences discussion of psychological factors. But:

- The CDC's position reflects the 2015 IOM report and the weight of evidence, not political pressure
- The 2021 NICE guideline explicitly acknowledges psychological wellbeing as part of holistic care while removing GET
- The article conflates "acknowledging psychological factors in illness experience" with "the illness IS psychological" — a category error that the patient community has been fighting for decades
- The mind-body proponents the article champions (Schubiner, Kennedy) argue that symptoms ARE created by the brain — this is a much stronger psychogenic claim than what the article's "just let us talk about it" framing suggests

## Author Background

**Alan Levinovitz** is a professor of **religion** at James Madison University. His previous book is *Natural: How Faith in Nature's Goodness Leads to Harmful Fads, Unjust Laws, and Flawed Science.* His forthcoming book (the article is effectively a promo for it) is titled *Demons by Another Name: Biology, Belief and the Stories That Make Us Sick.*

He co-authored a 2025 paper on "The Rumpelstiltskin Effect" in *BJPsych Bulletin* — arguing that clinical diagnostic labels can cause therapeutic harm. This intellectual framework (questioning whether diagnostic labels create or worsen illness) clearly shapes the article's thesis.

Levinovitz has previously posted that "60-80% of Long Covid is explicable" as psychogenic — a claim David Tuller calls unsupported "nonsense."

**This does not disqualify him from writing about Long COVID**, but it does mean readers should understand he is not a medical researcher evaluating evidence neutrally. He is a humanities scholar with a pre-existing thesis about diagnostic labels, promoting a book, writing for a general-interest magazine.

## Omissions of Consequence

1. **The Cell 2026 autoantibody paper** — causal proof published 2 weeks before the article; completely absent
2. **The NICE 2021 guideline withdrawal of GET** — the article mentions NICE but not that GET was withdrawn due to evidence of harm
3. **The PACE trial's pre-registered endpoint failure** — recovery rates of ~7–8% across all arms; the article only mentions the controversy, not the data
4. **Null objective outcomes in the CBT trial the BMJ review relies on** — body monitors showed no increase in activity
5. **The Finnish Gupta Program trial interim null results**
6. **The Lightning Process trial's methodological scandal**
7. **Schubiner's Sarno lineage** — the TMS framework's decades-long failure to produce biomarkers
8. **RECOVER's Neuro trial failure** — cognitive retraining didn't work
9. **Two-day CPET evidence** — objective, reproducible biomarker for PEM
10. **Any account of harm from brain retraining or mind-body interventions**

## What the Article Gets Right

To be fair:
- There truly are no FDA-approved drugs for Long COVID
- RECOVER's treatment trials have been disappointing
- The NASEM case definition is broad (Dr. Gaffney's "broadest definition" quote is fair)
- Some patients genuinely report benefit from brain retraining
- Online harassment of researchers is real and indefensible
- Prevalence estimates vary wildly across studies
- The PACE trial was genuinely methodologically flawed

## Culture War Dynamics

The article is a case study in how Long COVID discourse maps onto pre-existing culture war structures:

- **"Silenced truth-teller" framing:** The subtitle ("if only you were allowed to talk about it") positions the article as breaking a taboo. But mind-body approaches have been discussed extensively for years — in academic journals, clinical trials, patient forums, and previous media coverage.
- **The "both sides" trap:** By framing this as a "religious war" between biomedical and mind-body camps, the article creates false equivalence. One side has *Nature* and *Cell* publications demonstrating causal mechanisms. The other side's flagship intervention (PACE) failed its own pre-registered endpoints.
- **The politics of "belief":** The article repeatedly frames patient resistance as a matter of "belief" and "identity" — patients need to believe their illness is biological. This psychologizes patient advocacy rather than engaging with its evidence base.
- **Insurance implications:** As the Brainworks critique notes, the article's framing "feeds an existing insurance-denial infrastructure governed by ERISA 'objective evidence' requirements." If LC is a mind-body condition treatable by brain retraining, insurers can deny expensive biomedical testing and treatment.

## Recommendations

1. **Do not cite this article as an authoritative source** on Long COVID science. It is a narrative essay, not science journalism.
2. **If referencing the BMJ review**, note that it is actively contested, rests on single trials at high risk of bias, and contradicts NICE 2021 guidelines.
3. **The Cell 2026 autoantibody paper** is currently the strongest evidence for a biomedical mechanism and should be tracked.
4. **The RECOVER clinical trial disappointments** (Paxlovid, metformin as treatment) are real and fairly noted — but the article overstates the case that "nothing" has been found.
5. **Brain retraining may help some patients** — the absence of RCT evidence does not mean it never works — but the article's uncritical promotion goes well beyond the evidence.
