# OSHApunk



## A Factory Game About Building the Machine Without Breaking the Town



### Working Tagline



**Build the factory. Don’t break the town.**



Alternate goblin tagline:



**The belts must flow. The council has notes.**



---



## 1. Core Premise



**OSHApunk** is a nonviolent factory / civic infrastructure game set in a bounded district where the player must build and operate industrial systems inside a living community.



Unlike many factory games, the player is not an omnipotent extractor conquering an empty world. The player is part of a larger supply chain. The district has existing inputs, outputs, neighborhoods, traffic patterns, public infrastructure, regulatory bodies, legacy buildings, local personalities, and inconvenient little citizens with routines and complaints.



The central fantasy is not total control.



The central fantasy is:



> I am an infrastructure wizard making ugly compromises elegant.



The player builds belts, pipes, power systems, processing chains, logistics routes, transit interfaces, utility corridors, and production facilities. But every piece of infrastructure exists in a civic environment. People live here. They walk through here. They complain about noise, shadows, danger, ugly structures, blocked routes, pollution, and unsafe shortcuts. They may also appreciate a well-designed factory, adopt an ugly temporary bridge as a local landmark, or turn a noise wall into a mural.



The factory must grow, but the town must remain livable.



The game is not about eliminating friction. It is about civilizing friction.



---



## 2. The Big Difference From Traditional Factory Games



Traditional factory games often begin with a powerful fantasy:



> Here is land. Do as thou wilt.



The map may be hostile, but it is usually socially empty. If anything pushes back, it is often nature, monsters, resource scarcity, or logistics complexity. In Factorio, enemies attack the factory. In Satisfactory, the world is beautiful but ultimately indifferent to the player’s industrial sprawl. The main constraint is engineering optimization.



OSHApunk changes the premise:



> Here is land. People already live here. They have opinions. Also, the old bakery is in the way.



The player still solves production problems, but the problems are now embedded in civic life.



A belt is not just a belt. It may block a school route.



A pipe is not just a pipe. It may cast a shadow over the market.



A substation is not just a substation. It may stabilize the grid while making the nearby district angry.



A centralized megafactory is not just efficient. It is also a single point of failure, an inspection nightmare, a noise source, a traffic magnet, and a political disaster waiting to happen.



The core design idea:



> Factory layout should be shaped by the living environment, not merely imposed upon it.



---



## 3. Tone: Industrial Cozy, Not Industrial Grim



OSHApunk should not be grimdark industrial misery, but it also should not be frictionless utopia.



The desired tone is something like:



> Industrial cozy.



Not cozy as in pastel jam jars and sheep.



Cozy as in:



* the tram runs on time;

* the factory hums without deafening anyone;

* kids have safe crossings;

* the old bakery still exists somehow;

* waste heat warms apartments;

* the ugly temporary pipe bridge became a local landmark;

* people still complain, but mostly from inside a functioning place.



The end state should not be a strip-mined horror show with bug corpses outside the walls. It should be a reasonably harmonious civic-industrial situation. Not perfect. Never perfect. The player is always annoying someone. But the district works.



The victory condition is not universal happiness.



The victory condition is:



> Stable dissatisfaction.



Everyone has notes. Nobody is burning down the planning office.



---



## 4. Philosophical / Zeitgeist Targeting



OSHApunk fits a cultural moment where the fantasy of effortless individual escape is starting to feel thin.



For a long stretch, public imagination often leaned toward decentralization-as-lifestyle: everyone opens an Etsy store, installs solar panels, becomes a tiny sovereign brand, and opts out of ugly centralized systems. Infrastructure was treated as messy, suspect, bureaucratic, and aesthetically offensive.



But leaner times make background systems visible again.



Power generation matters. Water systems matter. Transit matters. Domestic manufacturing matters. Supply chains matter. Heat waves, fires, floods, grid instability, housing pressure, and migration matter. Jobs matter. Stability matters.



The question becomes less:



> Can we opt out of the ugly industrial world?



And more:



> Can we make the necessary systems less stupid, less brutal, and less brittle?



OSHApunk is about that question.



It is not anti-industry. It is not a return-to-nature fantasy. It is not “factory good, people irrelevant.” It is about mediating the old problems of industrialism rather than pretending they can be wished away.



A useful framing:



> The future is pipes, railings, substations, heat pumps, union complaints, storm drains, backup batteries, bus depots, noise walls, awkward compromises, and one beloved ugly overpass everyone pretends to hate.



The game’s emotional promise is that accommodating people is difficult, but not pointless. The little citizens are not just obstacles. They are noisy sensor data. They can be wrong, petty, contradictory, and infuriating. But they are also sometimes right. They reveal how infrastructure actually lands in lived space.



The player’s arc:



> “These people don’t understand throughput.”

>

> “Oh no. I was optimizing a subsystem and breaking the habitat.”



That is the philosophical heart of the game.



---



## 5. The Player Role



The player is not a mayor, not a god, not a warlord, and not a traditional city-builder planner.



The player is something closer to:



* site engineer;

* district infrastructure lead;

* industrial systems planner;

* production/logistics designer;

* safety compliance goblin;

* person with a build tool, a clipboard, and too many stakeholders.



The player does not directly command citizens. The player does not place houses and tell people where to live. The player does not bribe officials or buy silence. The player does not solve conflicts with money.



Instead, the player builds and adjusts physical systems.



The player’s tools are:



* routing;

* buffering;

* redundancy;

* safety;

* beautification;

* separation;

* access control;

* noise reduction;

* power stabilization;

* transit design;

* temporary works;

* inspection compliance;

* spatial compromise.



The game should make the player feel like they are responsible for a site, not ruling a map from heaven.



A useful design maxim:



> Make the player inhabit the site, not operate the ankles.



---



## 6. No Universal Money System



OSHApunk should avoid money as a universal currency, at least as a primary mechanic.



Once money becomes the dominant abstraction, the optimal solution risks becoming:



> build the ugly doom box;

> make enormous money;

> buy off everyone important;

> laugh from the top of the doom box while citizens complain ineffectively to a governing infrastructure you own.



That is a different game. It could be funny as a capitalist critique, but it is not the intended vibe.



OSHApunk should keep problems physical.



The real currencies are:



* space;

* noise;

* access;

* safety;

* trust;

* time;

* ugliness;

* disruption;

* redundancy;

* public patience;

* grid capacity;

* political tolerance;

* maintenance complexity.



The player cannot simply outspend consequences. They have to make the thing fit.



---



## 7. District Framing



The game should be district-scale, not planet-scale.



There is no endless terrain. No infinite resource patches. No “just build a giant iron mine far away.” The player manages a bounded district with existing input/output interfaces at the edges.



The player is part of a supply chain, not its totality.



The district might have:



* rail spurs;

* truck gates;

* port access;

* utility feeds;

* substations;

* municipal water;

* waste outflows;

* commuter routes;

* neighboring districts;

* upstream suppliers;

* downstream consumers;

* emergency service access;

* civic boundaries;

* legacy buildings;

* neighborhoods;

* public spaces.



The player can influence the edge numbers, but abstractly and slowly. They do not walk ten kilometers to claim another mine. They upgrade the rail siding, improve truck scheduling, stabilize a grid interconnect, add local storage, or harden a supply interface.



This avoids the standard factory-game expansion loop:



> Need more iron? Find bigger iron patch. Build longer belt. Strip-mine horizon. Repeat.



Instead:



> Here are the district’s constraints. Make it work here.



The district scale also makes place matter. The old market, canal bridge, tram depot, school route, hillside neighborhood, and cursed bakery become persistent facts. They are not disposable. The player must build around them or transform them carefully.



Good OSHApunk maps should develop memory.



The player should think:



> That stupid kink in the belt exists because of the frog pond.

> The distributed smelter exists because the main plant kept browning out the east district.

> The turbine hall façade exists because the residents fought me, and honestly they were right.

> The old warehouse shell is a pain to build inside, but it saved materials and became iconic.

> The overpass is still called The Vomit Viaduct, but it works.



---



## 8. Core Gameplay Triangle



The strategic triangle:



### Efficiency



Centralized, dense, cheap, high-throughput, easy to expand. The classic factory-game instinct.



### Resilience



Distributed, redundant, locally buffered, less vulnerable to inspections, outages, protests, traffic failures, or single-point breakdowns.



### Civic Acceptance



Safe, pretty, quiet, accessible, contextual, legible, politically survivable.



None of these should be purely good.



A beautiful distributed factory costs space and complexity.



A dense megafactory prints throughput until one inspection or grid issue shuts half the district down.



A civic-friendly design may be safer and more resilient but materially awkward.



The player’s job is to navigate tradeoffs, not find a single optimal blueprint.



---



## 9. Dynamic Nonviolent Pressure



OSHApunk should avoid combat as the primary pressure system.



The world pushes back through nonviolent, physical, civic, regulatory, and environmental change.



Possible pressure events:



* zoning law changes;

* new housing built near a facility;

* a school opens near a truck route;

* a market expands into a logistics corridor;

* new neighbor complaints;

* heat wave increases power demand;

* brownouts from regional grid instability;

* stormwater overflow;

* inspection shutdown;

* festival route temporarily blocks freight;

* transit route changes alter pedestrian flows;

* heritage board protects a building the player wanted to demolish;

* new safety standards require retrofits;

* residents object to noise or shadows;

* emergency services need clearer access;

* upstream supply becomes less stable;

* downstream output requirements change.



The key is that these pressures should feel like a living environment, not a disaster roulette machine.



The best failures are causal chains:



> Your iron plates line is down because an inspection closed the catwalk.

> The catwalk was closed because you routed high-pressure steam overhead.

> You routed steam overhead because the bakery refused relocation.

> The bakery refused relocation because your noise wall blocked its sign.

> The sign matters because the mayor promised “small business friendly industrial renewal.”

> Meanwhile, the copper line is fine, but nobody can reach the bathroom.



That is factory storytelling.



---



## 10. Citizens: Obstacles, Sensors, Stakeholders



The citizens are the soul risk of the game.



They cannot just be enemies with zoning opinions. That would be misanthropic. Funny for a moment, spiritually rotten after that.



The citizens should be:



* inconvenient because they are alive;

* sometimes wrong;

* sometimes petty;

* sometimes funny;

* sometimes hypocritical;

* sometimes absolutely correct;

* specific enough to care about;

* simple enough not to become RimWorld.



The player should not manage their lives. The player should design around the fact that lives are happening.



Citizens generate fuzzy requirements:



> “The new elevator is too loud.”

> “We want better transit but no tracks near the market.”

> “The factory should create jobs but not truck traffic.”

> “Can the power plant be moved farther away but still make electricity cheaper?”

> “Why are there pipes over the daycare?”

> “That conveyor bridge is ugly.”



Sometimes the aesthetic complaint points to a real mechanical issue. The ugly bridge might also block sightlines, cast shadows, funnel noise, or encourage unsafe shortcuts.



A key design principle:



> Public complaints are noisy sensor data.



Not always correct. Not meaningless.



Citizens should also visibly benefit from the player’s work. They should not only appear as complaint popups.



Positive feedback examples:



* a safe crossing becomes a school route;

* waste heat warms apartments;

* a repaired tram line changes foot traffic;

* the bakery that blocked a rail spur becomes a worker lunch hub;

* residents paint murals on a noise wall;

* a formerly hated pipe bridge becomes locally beloved;

* a district gains pride in a well-designed public works building.



The player needs moments of:



> Fine. I’m glad I didn’t bulldoze them.



---



## 11. OSHA / Safety as Real Gameplay



The OSHA element turns decorative industrial greebles into actual mechanics.



In many factory games:



> “Do I need railings?”

> “No, but they look nice.”



In OSHApunk:



> “Do I need railings?”

> “Your facility has been shut down because three interns fell into the copper manifold.”



Safety is not just punishment. It is a design language.



Safety systems might include:



* railings;

* marked crossings;

* lockout zones;

* emergency stops;

* warning lights;

* signage;

* machine spacing;

* ventilation;

* heat shielding;

* noise barriers;

* maintenance access;

* fire lanes;

* evacuation routes;

* safe pedestrian routing;

* guard fences;

* clearance zones;

* inspection walkways.



The game should reward design-for-misuse:



> Does the system still work when Bob climbs over the pipe because his ex is at the crosswalk?



This is an unexplored factory-game verb.



Not merely: does the machine work when used correctly?



But:



> Does the site remain safe and functional when tiny idiots, inspectors, commuters, delivery carts, protestors, and lunch traffic interact with it?



The final boss is not a giant alien queen. It is a clipboard.



---



## 12. Aesthetic Mechanics



OSHApunk gives players a mechanical reason to do what many factory players already want to do: build beautifully.



The efficient ugly box remains possible. It may even be the best short-term answer.



But it has consequences:



* more complaints;

* worse inspection outcomes;

* lower civic acceptance;

* higher fragility;

* single-point failure risk;

* traffic concentration;

* noise concentration;

* ugly skyline effects;

* poor public trust.



Aesthetic investment should not be purely cosmetic. It can be social infrastructure.



A good-looking factory might:



* reduce complaints;

* improve civic tolerance;

* make future permits easier;

* improve district pride;

* reduce protest intensity;

* turn infrastructure into landmarks;

* help citizens accept unavoidable disruption.



But pretty is not free. Beautification takes space, materials, planning, and sometimes throughput compromises.



The most interesting designs solve multiple problems at once:



* noise wall also supports solar panels;

* pedestrian overpass doubles as cable tray;

* park hides underground logistics tunnel;

* old warehouse becomes a legal shell around compact production;

* wastewater canal becomes coolant loop;

* train station roof becomes drone landing infrastructure;

* decorative clocktower is secretly the main control relay;

* civic mural wall also dampens generator noise.



This is the difference between complexity and tedium.



The game should not add “purple ore” just to make “purple plates.” It should add new verbs, new constraints, and new possibilities.

## 13. Art Direction: Budget Jetsons Public Works



The desired visual lane is bright but not childish.



Not industrial grime.



Not effortless future utopia.



Not Toon Town.



Something closer to:



> Jetsons on a lower budget, built by committees, with deferred maintenance and public comment periods.



Retrofuture infrastructure allows simplification without forcing full twentieth-century industrial realism.



The Jetsons had a conveyor-belt civilization: moving sidewalks, pneumatic tubes, automatic grooming, food machines, belt-based dog walking, flying cars, and domestic automation. The gag was that every minor inconvenience was solved by a machine, and the machine created a slightly dumber inconvenience.



OSHApunk can inherit that, but remove effortless abundance.



Possible aesthetic pillars:



* art deco public works;

* municipal retrofuture;

* bright safety signage;

* chunky voxel machines;

* enamel warning labels;

* color-coded utility corridors;

* curved substations;

* civic murals;

* ornamental industrial façades;

* bureaucratic grandeur;

* visible maintenance patches;

* legacy infrastructure;

* public transit stations;

* pneumatic tubes;

* moving sidewalks;

* conveyor sidewalks;

* compact utility machines;

* old and new systems awkwardly layered.



The world should feel like:



> The city of tomorrow arrived, but it arrived under budget pressure, in phases, with zoning constraints, and now the conveyor sidewalk union has notes.



Possible phrase:



> Municipal deco.



The “Department of Conveyor Safety” should look like it could be carved into a beautiful stone lintel above a pneumatic-tube office.

## 14. Visual Citizens



The citizens need to read as people, but they do not need to be human.



Actually, realistic humans are likely the worst option. They invite animation scrutiny, clothing variation, gait problems, scale problems, uncanny behavior, and the expectation of high-budget character systems.



The better rule:



> Citizens should be complex in behavior, simple in body.



Possible citizen forms:



### Mascot Blobs



Small squishy civic blobs with hats, props, speech bubbles, color variations, little faces, clipboards, lunchboxes, protest signs, umbrellas, and hard hats.



Pros:



* easy squash/stretch;

* readable silhouettes;

* emotionally expressive;

* cheap to render;

* animation can be procedural;

* easy to vary with props;

* cute enough to soften bureaucracy.



### Robot-Adjacent Civic Creatures



Post-human or non-human municipal beings with simple bodies, rolling/hopping/wobbling motion, modular parts, and bureaucratic personalities.



Pros:



* avoids human animation expectations;

* fits retrofuture;

* allows gliding, rolling, blinking, swiveling;

* safety culture can be funny.



Risk:



* may reduce vulnerability unless they clearly have lives, attachments, shops, neighborhoods, rituals, and preferences.



### Non-Humanoid Floating Aliens



Useful for avoiding walk cycles, but dangerous because they may break pathing and safety constraints. If everyone floats, road crossings and belt obstructions matter less. This requires replacement constraints like airflow, humidity, nesting zones, or vertical clearance.



Likely best starting point:



> Squashy 3D mascot citizens, not sprites and not skinned humanoids.



Animation can be transform-based:



* body squash/stretch;

* hop/slide locomotion;

* lean into velocity;

* wobble props;

* face panel changes;

* tiny emote bubbles;

* startled pop;

* complaining bounce;

* clipboard inspection stance.



No bones. No knees. No mercy.

## 15. Camera / Presentation



First-person should likely be avoided for the initial version.



First-person adds enormous production complexity:



* looking up;

* ceilings;

* skies;

* interior cameras;

* vertical traversal;

* occlusion;

* collision;

* placement precision;

* animation scrutiny;

* art detail demands;

* scale issues.



An isometric/oblique perspective avoids most of that. No looking up means no ceilings and no skies. It also makes simple art more viable.



Pure strategy view, however, risks pushing the player into a god-game mindset. The citizens become ants. The district becomes a spreadsheet.



Recommended compromise:



> Grounded isometric / oblique builder with a visible player character or site presence.



The character should provide locality and presence, not arbitrary friction.



Good version:



> You can design broadly from a tablet/drone/build overlay, but some problems only become legible when you visit the site.



Bad version:



> You must stand within eight meters to place every pipe.



The player character is a sensor, not a leash.



Suggested modes:



### Normal Site View



Oblique/isometric camera. Player sees nearby citizens, belts, roads, buildings, and infrastructure. Camera can pan, rotate, tilt within limits, and zoom.



### Build / Planning Overlay



A tablet or drone mode for placing blueprints, inspecting overlays, safety maps, noise maps, access routes, complaints, and production chains.



### Inspection / Walkthrough Mode



Closer grounded camera for diagnosing site problems and feeling scale. Not necessarily first-person. This is emotional and diagnostic, not the main input mode.



Camera constraints:



* rotate around vertical axis;

* tilt within a controlled range;

* no under-camera;

* no true horizon view;

* no ceilings;

* no sky dependency;

* cutaway/fade buildings when blocking;

* vertical layers via slicing/ghosting.



Maxim:



> Make the player inhabit the site, not operate the ankles.

## 16. Technology / Production Direction



Potential technical architecture discussed:



* fast voxel renderer in Electron;

* browser/Electron UI for complex overlays, inspectors, panels, complaint logs, planning tools;

* C# sidecar for town simulation / civic logic on another thread or process;

* render world and UI separated from simulation systems;

* district-scale maps rather than endless terrain;

* simple 3D blob citizens using instanced meshes / voxel parts / procedural animation;

* avoid skinned mesh crowds;

* avoid directional sprite explosion;

* avoid first-person art burden.



The UI burden is large. This game needs maps, overlays, reports, queues, complaints, safety audits, production diagrams, citizen flows, district metrics, and planning tools. Electron may be a reasonable choice precisely because the UI is browser-shaped.



The sim side should keep the civic goblin logic away from the render loop.



Citizen rendering should likely use LODs:



* close: simple 3D blob with face, props, squash/stretch;

* medium: instanced simplified blob with fewer props;

* far: symbol/dot/crowd flow marker;

* overlay: citizens become readable civic flows, risks, and complaints.



Avoid the “zillion skinned mesh renderers” road. That path is madness.

## 17. Relationship to Existing Games



### Satisfactory



Inspiration point for satisfying factory construction, spatial logistics, and player desire to make beautiful factories. But OSHApunk should avoid first-person burden and endless extraction sprawl.



### Factorio



Inspiration point for factory pressure and system clarity. But OSHApunk should replace bug warfare with civic/environmental pressure.



Tower defense pressure often collapses into another production sink:



> automate ammo → build perimeter → repair occasionally → scale ammo → bigger perimeter.



In OSHApunk, the pressure is more interesting because it attacks the player’s favorite factory fantasy: total authorship.



### RimWorld



Inspiration point for bobble people with weird little dramas, but without direct colony management. Citizens are environmental and civic agents, not pawns.



Concept:



> RimWorld without the city-builder mechanics.

> Little bobble people everywhere getting underfoot while you build a factory.



### X-COM: Apocalypse



A loose ancestor: city-scale strategic layer, organizations, dynamic politics, factions, and a detailed urban environment replacing a global map. Not cozy and about alien warfare, but relevant as an old example of shrinking the strategic scope from world to city.



Useful inheritance:



> Replace the abstract global layer with a specific city/district full of institutions.



OSHApunk’s divergence:



> Not city as warzone. District as living civic organism.



### SimCity / City Builders



OSHApunk should not become a traditional city builder. The player does not zone housing, manage tax rates, or command citizens. The city is not the player’s toy. The district is a living constraint field.

## 18. Possible Campaign / Scenario Structure



Instead of one endless world, the game could use bounded districts with different civic-industrial personalities.



Examples:



### Old Mill Town



Legacy factories, heritage buildings, old canals, tight streets, strong local identity.



### Dense Port District



Freight bottlenecks, truck traffic, dock schedules, noise conflicts, port authority requirements.



### Hillside Utility District



Water pressure, landslide risk, awkward elevation, scenic complaints, difficult transit.



### Inland Heat District



Cooling demand, brownouts, solar potential, grid instability, heat-safe public routes.



### Floodplain District



Stormwater, raised infrastructure, evacuation routes, wetland buffers, flood-resilient logistics.



### Post-Industrial Downtown



Warehouse reuse, clean manufacturing, mixed-use conflict, historic façades, limited freight access.



### Rail Yard / Junction Town



Scheduling, noise, grade crossings, hazardous cargo routing, transit conflicts.



Each district should have character, constraints, and persistent local landmarks.

## 20. Design Rules



### 1. The player solves with physical systems, not abstract money.



The answer to a complaint should usually be something built, rerouted, buffered, shielded, beautified, or made redundant.



### 2. Citizens are not enemies.



They are stakeholders and sensors. They can be frustrating but should remain worth accommodating.



### 3. Beauty should have mechanical value.



Aesthetic design is social infrastructure.



### 4. The district should have memory.



Compromises should persist and become part of the map’s story.



### 5. Friction should be legible.



The player should understand why people are upset, why inspections fail, why routes break, and why systems cascade.



### 6. Avoid universal optimality.



Centralization, distribution, beautification, redundancy, and speed all have tradeoffs.



### 7. Keep the world bounded.



No infinite resource frontier. No escaping constraints by sprawling forever.



### 8. Nonviolent pressure should feel serious.



The game does not need combat to create stakes.



### 9. Make accommodation satisfying.



The player should feel pride when the messy compromise works.



### 10. The best solutions solve multiple problems.



A safe route that also carries cables. A park that hides logistics. A noise wall that becomes a mural. An old building that becomes a production shell.

## 21. Achievement / Marketing Hooks



### Beloved Eyesore



Turn an ugly temporary structure into a protected local landmark.



This is the key achievement. Do not lose it.



It is the kind of achievement popup that markets the game when players post screenshots of absurd infrastructure they are weirdly proud of.



### Other Achievement Ideas



**Stable Dissatisfaction**

Keep all districts below crisis level for a full year.



**Technically a Park**

Hide 500 meters of logistics infrastructure under public green space.



**The Vomit Viaduct**

Have citizens successfully rename one of your structures.



**Railings Are Content**

Pass an inspection after installing 200 safety barriers.



**Everyone Hates This Less Now**

Resolve a five-faction complaint without demolishing anything.



**No Knees, No Mercy**

Support a large citizen population with no humanoid animation system.



**Clipboard Final Boss**

Pass a full district safety audit.



**Public Comment Period**

Survive a major infrastructure redesign without shutting down production.



**This Was Temporary**

Keep a temporary structure in service long enough for citizens to demand its preservation.



**Not In My Bus Corridor**

Reroute a major logistics line around a protected civic route.

## 23. The Emotional End State



A completed OSHApunk district should not look pristine.



It should look lived-in.



The player should see:



* old infrastructure retrofitted around new systems;

* belts bent around beloved buildings;

* noise walls covered in murals;

* service tunnels under parks;

* ugly but useful elevated corridors;

* safe pedestrian bridges;

* compact distributed workshops;

* redundant grid buffers;

* a bakery somehow still operating beside a logistics artery;

* citizens taking routes the player designed for them;

* a few ongoing complaints that will never fully go away.



The player should feel:



> This is messy.

> This is compromised.

> This works.

> I’m glad I didn’t just build the doom box.



That is the heart.

## 24. One-Sentence Pitch



**OSHApunk is a nonviolent factory game about building industrial systems inside a living district, where efficiency, safety, beauty, resilience, and civic patience all compete — and the best victory is a beloved eyesore everyone can live with.**
