# Obscure Causes of Severe Chronic Constipation with Abdominal Distension: A Differential Diagnosis Deep-Dive

**Date:** 2026-06-21  
**Researcher:** researcher  
**Clinical scenario:** Adult with years-long severe intestinal/stomach distension (visible belly distension when flaring), frequent lengthy bouts of constipation, 3 incomplete colonoscopies despite proper prep, multiple specialist visits with no clear diagnosis. Standard diet/lifestyle/medical solutions have been tried repeatedly.

**Objective:** Identify non-obvious, under-recognized, or specialist-level conditions and diagnostic avenues that may have been missed. This is NOT a substitute for medical advice — it's a research map for discussion with a qualified gastroenterologist, ideally a neurogastroenterology/motility subspecialist.

---

## Executive Summary

The combination of **severe visible distension + chronic constipation + 3 incomplete colonoscopies despite prep** is a specific clinical pattern that narrows the differential substantially. The incomplete colonoscopies are a significant clue — in the literature, the most common cause is a tortuous/redundant colon (30% of incomplete scopes), which itself is strongly associated with slow-transit constipation and certain connective tissue disorders.

Six obscure but testable conditions merit investigation, ordered roughly from most to least likely to have been missed by a general gastroenterologist:

1. **Abdomino-phrenic dyssynergia (APD)** — explains the extreme visible distension without requiring excess gas; treatable with biofeedback breathing therapy
2. **Intestinal methanogen overgrowth (IMO)** — methane-producing archaea slow gut transit; highly treatable (rifaximin + neomycin)
3. **Pelvic floor dyssynergia / outlet obstruction** — muscles paradoxically contract during attempted defecation; common, treatable with biofeedback
4. **Autoimmune GI dysmotility (AGID)** — antibodies attacking gut nerves; testable via blood panel, treatable with immunotherapy
5. **Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (hEDS/HSD)** — connective tissue disorder with 73% constipation rate; explains redundant colon, motility problems, multi-system involvement
6. **Short-segment adult Hirschsprung's disease** — missing ganglion cells in a short rectal segment; requires full-thickness biopsy to diagnose

## 1. The Incomplete Colonoscopy Clue

Three incomplete colonoscopies despite proper bowel preparation is not normal and should not be dismissed. The published literature on incomplete colonoscopy gives these causes:

| Cause | Frequency | Relevance to this case |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| Tortuous/redundant colon | ~30% | **Highly relevant** — associated with chronic constipation, EDS |
| Pain / inadequate sedation | ~16% | Possible but less likely with repeated attempts |
| Poor bowel preparation | ~11% | Ruled out by history ("full prep being done") |
| Diverticular disease | ~6% | Would have been noted on prior scopes |
| Obstructing lesion | ~5% | Would have been noted if present |

A redundant/tortuous colon is an anatomically extra-long colon that loops and twists to fit inside the abdomen. This makes scope passage technically difficult AND contributes to slow transit constipation (longer path = longer transit time). Cleveland Clinic notes it's "related to constipation and slow intestinal transit time."

**What to ask about:** Do the colonoscopy reports mention "tortuous colon," "redundant colon," "difficult scope passage," "looping," or "could not reach cecum"? If so, this is a documented anatomical contributor.

## 2. Abdomino-Phrenic Dyssynergia (APD) — The "Invisible" Distension Cause

**This is the single most likely condition to have been missed.** APD was only characterized in the last decade and many general gastroenterologists are unfamiliar with it.

### What it is
A gut-brain coordination disorder where the diaphragm and abdominal wall move in the **opposite** direction of normal during a bloating episode:
- **Normal response to gas:** Diaphragm relaxes (rises), abdominal wall contracts (tightens) → gas is accommodated without visible distension
- **APD response:** Diaphragm contracts (pushes down), abdominal wall relaxes (bulges out) → visible belly distension, often dramatic

### Why it explains this case
- The distension is visible, mechanical, and real — not "in the patient's head"
- CT imaging studies show that only ~25% of patients with functional bloating have excess intestinal gas during a distension episode. The distension comes from muscle coordination, not gas volume
- It frequently co-occurs with constipation-predominant IBS and functional constipation
- It produces the exact symptom described: "extreme distention when flaring"

### Diagnosis and treatment
- Diagnosed by clinical pattern + exclusion of other causes
- **Treatment: biofeedback-based breathing therapy** (diaphragmatic breathing retraining, abdominal wall coordination exercises) — this is NOT a drug, it's physical therapy
- Central neuromodulators (low-dose tricyclics, SNRIs) can help in refractory cases
- Rome Foundation and major motility centers now recognize this

### Sources
- PMC9053509 (2022): "A Practical Approach to the Diagnosis and Treatment of Abdominal Bloating and Distension" — identifies APD as one of 5 key etiologies
- PMC9810002 (2023): "Abdominophrenic Dyssynergia: A Narrative Review" — comprehensive overview
- Medicine Today (2025): "Not just gas: rethinking abdominal distension in clinical practice"

## 3. Intestinal Methanogen Overgrowth (IMO) / Methane SIBO

### What it is
Overgrowth of methane-producing archaea (primarily *Methanobrevibacter smithii*) in the small intestine. Methane is a potent paralytic to gut smooth muscle — it directly slows intestinal transit.

### Why it explains this case
- Methane → slowed transit → constipation → more time for bacterial fermentation → more methane → worse constipation (vicious cycle)
- Severe bloating and distension are hallmark symptoms
- Methane producers also cause the belly distension pattern
- Standard constipation treatments (fiber, laxatives) often make IMO **worse** because they provide more substrate for methanogens

### Diagnosis
- **Lactulose or glucose breath test** measuring hydrogen AND methane. Must specifically measure methane — many older SIBO tests only measured hydrogen
- Methane-positive: ≥10 ppm at any point during the test
- This is non-invasive, inexpensive, and widely available

### Treatment
- **Rifaximin (550 mg TID × 14 days) + Neomycin (500 mg BID × 14 days)** — combination achieves 85% clinical response vs 56% for rifaximin alone (Pimentel et al., 2010)
- Methane eradication: 87% with combination vs 28% with rifaximin alone
- Elemental diet as alternative/rescue therapy
- Prokinetic agents post-treatment to prevent recurrence
- This is a **highly treatable** condition that can produce dramatic improvement

### Sources
- Pimentel et al. 2010 (PMID 19996983): combination rifaximin + neomycin study
- North American Consensus on Breath Testing guidelines
- Gastroenterology & Hepatology (March 2023): IMO as distinct entity from hydrogen SIBO

## 4. Pelvic Floor Dyssynergia (Outlet Obstruction)

### What it is
The pelvic floor muscles and anal sphincter **paradoxically contract** instead of relaxing during attempted defecation. The patient is literally pushing against a closed door. This can cause:
- Sensation of incomplete evacuation
- Straining
- Severe constipation
- Secondary abdominal distension from retained stool and gas

### Why it's commonly missed
- Standard colonoscopy does not evaluate pelvic floor function
- Patients often report they "push hard" but don't realize they're pushing against contracted muscles
- Digital rectal exam by an experienced examiner has 75% sensitivity, 87% specificity — but many GIs skip it or don't test specifically for dyssynergia (asking patient to bear down during the exam)

### Diagnosis
- **Anorectal manometry** with balloon expulsion test — the gold standard
- Defecography (MR or barium) — shows the abnormal muscle pattern during attempted defecation
- Surface EMG biofeedback assessment

### Treatment
- **Pelvic floor biofeedback therapy** — 70–80% response rate, durable improvement
- This is NOT surgery, NOT drugs — it's neuromuscular retraining with a specialized physical therapist

### Source
- IFFGD: Dyssynergic Defecation treatment overview
- Rao et al.: diagnostic criteria and manometry protocols

## 5. Autoimmune Gastrointestinal Dysmotility (AGID)

### What it is
Autoantibodies attack the autonomic nervous system controlling gut motility, particularly antibodies against ganglionic acetylcholine receptors (anti-gAChR). This can cause:
- Severe gastroparesis
- Intestinal pseudo-obstruction
- Chronic constipation
- Diffuse GI dysmotility

### Why it fits this case
- Can present in adults with no prior history
- Can be idiopathic or paraneoplastic (associated with occult malignancy, particularly small-cell lung cancer, thymoma)
- Produces exactly the "pseudo-obstruction" picture: looks like a blockage but no mechanical obstruction found
- Can be triggered by viral infection (including post-COVID cases documented)

### Diagnosis
- **Mayo Clinic GID2 panel** (serum): tests for anti-gAChR, anti-VGKC, anti-VGCC, anti-Hu, anti-CRMP-5, and other paraneoplastic antibodies
- If positive → CT chest/abdomen/pelvis to rule out occult malignancy
- Gastric emptying study, whole-gut transit study to document dysmotility pattern

### Treatment
- If paraneoplastic: treat underlying malignancy
- If idiopathic autoimmune: IVIG, plasmapheresis, rituximab, or steroid-sparing immunosuppression
- Prokinetic agents for symptom management

### Sources
- Mayo Clinic: Autoimmune GI Dysmotility Evaluation (GID2 test overview)
- PMC2741093: "Serological Profiles Aiding the Diagnosis of Autoimmune GI Dysmotility"
- Post-COVID AGID cases documented (Neurogastroenterology & Motility, 2022)

## 6. Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome / Hypermobility Spectrum Disorder

### What it is
A group of heritable connective tissue disorders. The hypermobile type (hEDS) is the most common and frequently undiagnosed in adults. GI manifestations are extremely prevalent but under-recognized.

### GI manifestations in hEDS/HSD
| Symptom | Prevalence in hEDS | Control prevalence |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Constipation | **73%** | 16% |
| Abdominal pain | 69% | 27% |
| Diarrhea | 47% | 9% |
| IBS-like symptoms | 75% | — |
| Nausea | 42% | — |
| Functional dyspepsia | Common | — |

### Why it explains this case
- **Redundant/tortuous colon** is more common in connective tissue disorders (tissue laxity → elongated colon with more loops)
- **Visceral ptosis** (organ drooping) can cause kinking, slow transit
- **Dysautonomia** commonly co-occurs → affects gut motility
- **Mast cell activation** commonly co-occurs → gut inflammation, bloating
- The multi-system nature (fatigue, joint pain, easy bruising, POTS symptoms, etc.) often goes unrecognized as a unified diagnosis
- The 2017 diagnostic criteria for hEDS are clinical — no genetic test required

### What to look for in history
- Joint hypermobility (Beighton score)
- Easy bruising, poor wound healing
- Chronic pain, fatigue
- Dizziness on standing (POTS/dysautonomia)
- Family history of similar issues

### What to do
- Evaluation by a rheumatologist or geneticist familiar with hEDS/HSD
- If confirmed → GI management shifts: gentler approach, awareness of tissue fragility, autonomic management
- Source: PMC9544979 (2022): "Hypermobile Ehlers–Danlos syndrome and disorders of the gastrointestinal tract: What the gastroenterologist needs to know"

## 7. Short-Segment / Ultrashort-Segment Adult Hirschsprung's Disease

### What it is
Congenital absence of ganglion cells in the distal colon, usually diagnosed in infancy. BUT short-segment and ultrashort-segment variants can escape diagnosis until adulthood. The aganglionic segment fails to relax, creating a functional obstruction.

### Why it fits
- Lifelong constipation history (often "since childhood")
- Severe distension
- Normal-appearing colon above the affected segment
- Can cause incomplete colonoscopy if the segment is in a difficult location
- Many published case reports of adults diagnosed in their 20s–50s

### Diagnosis
- **Full-thickness rectal biopsy** (not mucosal — must include submucosa to evaluate ganglion cells)
- Anorectal manometry: absent rectoanal inhibitory reflex (RAIR) is the classic finding
- Barium enema may show a narrow distal segment with proximal dilation (transition zone)

### Treatment
- Surgical: pull-through procedure (Duhamel, Soave, or Swenson techniques)
- Internal anal sphincter myectomy for ultrashort segment
- Results in adults are generally good when properly diagnosed

### Sources
- PMC11870301 (2025): "Delayed diagnosis and management of adult Hirschsprung's disease: Case report and literature review"
- Multiple adult case reports from 1978–2024 document chronic constipation as the presenting symptom

## 8. Visceral Myopathy (Genetic)

### What it is
Genetic degeneration of smooth muscle in the GI tract, most commonly caused by **ACTG2 mutations** (smooth muscle gamma-actin). Accounts for ~60% of visceral myopathy cases. Autosomal dominant inheritance.

### Presentation
- Chronic intestinal pseudo-obstruction (CIPO)
- Severe constipation refractory to all standard treatments
- Abdominal distension
- Often associated with urinary bladder involvement (megacystis)
- May have family history of similar GI issues

### Diagnosis
- Genetic testing: ACTG2 sequencing (and broader smooth muscle myopathy panels)
- Full-thickness intestinal biopsy showing smooth muscle degeneration/vacuolization
- May require surgical biopsy if endoscopic samples insufficient

### Source
- PMC8285581 (2021): "Visceral myopathy: clinical syndromes, genetics, pathophysiology"

## 9. Diagnostic Tests That May Not Have Been Done

Many of these conditions require specific tests beyond standard colonoscopy and basic labs:

| Test | What It Diagnoses | Availability |
|------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Lactulose/glucose breath test (H₂ + CH₄)** | IMO / methane SIBO | Widely available, non-invasive |
| **Sitzmarks colonic transit study** | Slow-transit vs normal-transit constipation | Widely available, inexpensive |
| **Anorectal manometry + balloon expulsion** | Pelvic floor dyssynergia, Hirschsprung's (absent RAIR) | Available at motility centers |
| **Wireless motility capsule (SmartPill)** | Regional transit times, pressure patterns | Available at motility centers |
| **AGID antibody panel (Mayo GID2)** | Autoimmune GI dysmotility | Send-out lab, blood draw |
| **Full-thickness rectal biopsy** | Hirschsprung's disease (aganglionosis) | Surgical pathology |
| **ACTG2 / smooth muscle myopathy genetic panel** | Visceral myopathy | Genetic testing labs |
| **MR defecography** | Pelvic floor dyssynergia, rectocele, intussusception | Radiology |
| **CT enterography** | Structural lesions, inflammation | Widely available |
| **Beighton score / EDS clinical assessment** | hEDS/HSD screening | Rheumatology / genetics |

## 10. The Specialist to See

If this person has been seeing general gastroenterologists, the next step is a **neurogastroenterology and motility specialist** at a tertiary academic center. These are gastroenterologists with subspecialty training in gut motility disorders who have access to:
- Anorectal manometry
- Colonic manometry
- Wireless motility capsule
- Full-thickness biopsy capability
- Breath testing labs
- Biofeedback therapy programs

Major U.S. motility centers include: Mayo Clinic (Rochester, Scottsdale, Jacksonville), Johns Hopkins, Cleveland Clinic, UCLA, University of Michigan, Massachusetts General, Temple University, Augusta University.

## 11. Practical Discussion Points for Next Doctor Visit

Based on this research, reasonable questions to ask:

1. "My colonoscopies were incomplete despite proper prep — do the reports mention a tortuous or redundant colon? Could that be contributing to my symptoms?"

2. "Has anyone done a colonic transit study (Sitzmarks) to determine if I have slow-transit constipation?"

3. "Could this be a pelvic floor coordination problem? Would anorectal manometry help?"

4. "Would a lactulose breath test for methane overgrowth be appropriate? I've read methane can cause severe constipation."

5. "Has anyone considered autoimmune causes of gut dysmotility? Is the Mayo GID2 antibody panel something to check?"

6. "My visible belly distension is extreme when I flare — could this be abdomino-phrenic dyssynergia? Is biofeedback breathing therapy an option?"

7. "Should I be evaluated for Ehlers-Danlos syndrome or a hypermobility spectrum disorder, given the GI issues plus [any other symptoms: joint flexibility, easy bruising, etc.]?"

8. "Would a referral to a neurogastroenterology/motility center be appropriate at this point?"

## 13. Limitations and Caveats

- This is NOT medical advice. It's a research map for informed discussion with a qualified physician.
- The conditions discussed are rare to very rare. But when standard evaluations repeatedly fail, rare diagnoses become proportionally more likely.
- Some of these conditions (visceral myopathy, severe CIPO) have limited treatment options even when diagnosed. But some (IMO, pelvic floor dyssynergia, APD) are highly treatable and may produce dramatic improvement.
- I cannot assess this specific person's history, physical exam, prior workup details, or other symptoms that would refine the differential. This is why the motility specialist referral is the key recommendation.
