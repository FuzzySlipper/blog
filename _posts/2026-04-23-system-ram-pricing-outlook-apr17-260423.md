# System RAM Pricing Outlook: DIMM vs SO-DIMM (April 2026 Through 2028)

*Compiled April 17, 2026. Focus: current U.S. pricing for desktop DIMMs and laptop/mini-PC SO-DIMMs, how rising DRAM costs are flowing into new-system pricing, and whether waiting a few months is likely to help.*

---

## Executive Take

The short answer is:

- **RAM prices are still structurally bad in April 2026**
- **SO-DIMM is the more fragile market**
- **desktop DIMM pricing is ugly but sometimes less bad than OEM/system pricing**
- **waiting a few months is not a strategy I would count on for a needed replacement in 2026**

The most important distinction is between:

1. **chip and contract pricing**, which still point upward
2. **retail/module pricing**, which is volatile and can temporarily dip
3. **new-system pricing**, where OEMs often apply far worse memory markups than the DIY market

My current base view:

- **rest of 2026:** RAM remains expensive and erratic; some spot softness or temporary deal windows can happen, but there is no strong reason to expect a stable downtrend over the next few months
- **2027:** some relief is possible, but much less certain than a normal cycle because AI, sovereign-AI, and server demand are still crowding the market
- **2028:** better chance of meaningful easing, but not necessarily a return to late-2025 lows

So if someone has a surprise RAM failure in 2026, the practical advice is:

- **buy when you find a reasonable price from a real in-stock seller**
- **do not assume waiting 2-3 months will clearly improve the situation**

## What The Underlying Market Is Doing

TrendForce’s April data still points to a tight DRAM market:

- On **April 17, 2026**, TrendForce showed **DDR5 16Gb spot average at 38.50**, up from **37.43 on April 15** and **39.40 on March 12** earlier in the cycle.
- On **April 7, 2026**, TrendForce said **consumer DRAM contract prices** were expected to rise **45-50% QoQ in 2Q26**.
- On **March 31, 2026**, TrendForce’s contract report said supply constraints were driving **surging PC DRAM contract prices**, while weak retail sales caused spot prices to fall away from contract trends.
- On **March 18, 2026**, Micron said DRAM and NAND remain supply-constrained and that tight conditions should persist **beyond calendar 2026**.

Interpretation:

- The market is not showing clean normalization.
- The most likely pattern is **volatile retail/module pricing on top of an upward or at least stubbornly elevated contract environment**.
- That means a few weeks of better-looking listings do not necessarily imply a durable downtrend.

## Current Retail Snapshot: DDR5 DIMM vs SO-DIMM

Retail RAM pricing is now highly fragmented. One of the clearest practical lessons is:

- **do not trust stale MSRP pages**
- **do not treat an out-of-stock “price” as a real price**
- **always check whether the seller is the retailer itself or a marketplace seller**

Observed U.S. examples:

- Best Buy showed the **Crucial 32GB kit (2x16GB) DDR5-5600 SO-DIMM** at **$323.99** on a product page crawled **April 15, 2026**, sold out, with more buying options up to **$429.99**
- Best Buy also showed the same product through marketplace sellers at **$399.00**, **$437.99**, and even **$874.04**, all in April 2026 crawls
- Newegg showed the same kit at **$361.99** from marketplace sellers in a page opened April 17, 2026
- Best Buy’s **Crucial 64GB kit (2x32GB) DDR5-5600 SO-DIMM** was **$599.99** on April 15-17, 2026, with third-party offers as high as **$1,225.02**

Interpretation:

- SO-DIMM is not just expensive; it is **stock-sensitive and prone to extreme seller dispersion**.
- This fits your mini-PC experience very well: a machine can look “back to normal” because one seller shows the old MSRP while real in-stock supply is much higher or absent.

## Desktop DIMM: still expensive, but a bit less trapped

Observed U.S. examples:

- Best Buy showed the **Crucial 32GB kit (2x16GB) DDR5-5600 UDIMM** at **$335.99** on April 15-17, 2026 crawls
- Best Buy showed the **Crucial 64GB kit (2x32GB) DDR5-5600 UDIMM** at **$654.99**
- TrendForce’s **module spot price** for **DDR5 UDIMM 16GB 4800/5600** was **222.50** as of **April 6, 2026**
- Newegg marketplace listings for some 32GB desktop DDR5 kits were lower, in the **$99.50-$157.92** range for one Crucial 32GB DDR5-5600 kit page, which illustrates how chaotic the channel is rather than proving the market is healthy

Interpretation:

- Desktop DIMM is still under pressure, but the DIY desktop market has a better chance of finding a tolerable deal than the SO-DIMM market.
- The problem is less “no RAM exists” and more “pricing is inconsistent, stock is uneven, and good deals are not reliable enough to build a timing strategy around.”

## One Important Practical Point: SO-DIMM Has Probably Lost The “Wait For A Sale” Character

There is a strong anecdotal and retail-page case that SO-DIMM got much worse than many buyers expected.

The clearest example from available crawls:

- Best Buy showed the **same Crucial 32GB DDR5-5600 SO-DIMM kit** at **$104.99** in an older crawl from roughly **October 2025**
- that same product now shows real in-stock pricing around **$323.99-$399.00**, with some marketplace listings far above that

Interpretation:

- That is not a normal seasonal fluctuation.
- It suggests that for laptop/mini-PC users, the old instinct of “wait a few weeks for RAM to go on sale” is much less reliable than it used to be.

## How This Is Flowing Into New System Prices

RAM pricing is hitting new systems in two different ways:

### 1. OEM configuration premiums are brutal

Examples:

- Dell’s current U.S. **Pro Max Micro** configurator shows **32GB DDR5-5600 non-ECC** as a **+$540** option and **64GB DDR5-5600 non-ECC** as **+$1,340**
- HP’s **Z2 Mini G1a** configurator shows jumps of roughly **+$1,247 to +$1,697** when moving from 32GB to 64GB depending on configuration

Interpretation:

- OEM memory pricing was always worse than DIY, but the spread is now wide enough that buying a lower-RAM system and upgrading yourself is often the only rational path if the RAM is socketed.

### 2. Consumer systems are seeing price spikes, stock gaps, and deceptive old listings

This is consistent with your mini-PC example:

- a system bought with **32GB DDR5 SO-DIMM** for **$550** in **November 2025**
- later appearing near **$1,000**
- then coming back in stock around **$740**

That pattern is exactly what this market would produce:

- channel inventory sells through
- replacement stock lands at much higher component cost
- some pages continue displaying old prices with no real inventory
- later supply waves come back below the peak panic print, but not back to the old baseline

This is also consistent with current vendor behavior. For example, GEEKOM’s current U.S. mini-PC pages still show 32GB DDR5-class mini PCs in the rough **$619-$899** band depending on CPU and storage, which is clearly above the old “good value” mini-PC norm.

## Are RAM Prices Likely To Come Back Down?

## Over the next few months

### My view

- **Not in a way I would bet on**

Why:

- contract pricing still points up
- suppliers remain supply-constrained
- Micron says tightness extends beyond calendar 2026
- SO-DIMM and mini-PC channels are especially vulnerable to stock gaps and seller markups

So if someone asks:

- “Should I wait until summer 2026 for a needed RAM replacement?”

My answer is:

- **Only if you can comfortably live without it**
- **Otherwise, probably not**

## Over 2027

### My view

- **possible improvement, but not guaranteed**

Why:

- some new supply can arrive
- demand could cool if AI capex slows
- but sovereign AI and inference buildout could also keep conventional DDR and NAND tighter for longer

That means:

- **2027 is a maybe**
- **not a promise**

## Over 2028

### My view

- **best chance of broad easing**

Why:

- this is when more new capacity has a better shot at mattering
- it is also far enough out that a slowdown in AI spending could actually work through the system

But I would still not assume:

- a full return to the late-2025 “cheap DDR5” world

The most realistic optimism is:

- **better than 2026**
- **not necessarily back to old lows**

## Buying Guidance

## If RAM fails unexpectedly in 2026

- Buy from a real in-stock seller once you find an acceptable price.
- Do not spend months waiting for obvious relief.
- Prioritize compatibility and seller credibility over trying to nail the absolute bottom.

## If buying for a mini-PC or laptop with SO-DIMM

- Treat SO-DIMM as the riskier category.
- If you know you will need an upgrade, buying sooner is more defensible than waiting.
- Do not trust “old MSRP” if the product is out of stock.

## If buying for a desktop with standard DIMMs

- You have a better chance of finding temporary deals.
- Compare multiple channels carefully because desktop DIMM pricing is more fragmented.
- If the need is not urgent, it can make sense to watch for a few weeks.
- If the need is urgent, the expected gain from waiting a few months is still limited.

## If buying a new system

- Avoid paying OEM memory upgrade prices if the system has user-replaceable RAM.
- Prefer lower-RAM configurations plus aftermarket upgrade when possible.
- Be especially skeptical of mini-PC and laptop pages showing attractive prices when actual in-stock configurations are thin.

---

## Bottom-Line Judgments

1. **System RAM is still in a structurally bad market in April 2026.**
2. **SO-DIMM is more fragile than desktop DIMM and more prone to ugly stock-driven spikes.**
3. **The next few months do not look like a reliable waiting window for people who actually need RAM.**
4. **2027 may improve, but it is not dependable enough to base an urgent replacement strategy on.**
5. **2028 is the first horizon where broad easing looks more plausible than wishful.**
6. **For new systems, OEM RAM markups are often worse than DIY module pricing, so self-upgrade remains the best defense where possible.**
